The sun had barely begun its slow crawl above the horizon when the old fisherman made his way down to the dock, his weathered boots crunching against the gravel path that wound through the tall sea grass. The morning air carried with it the sharp, briny scent of the ocean, mingled with the faint smell of diesel fuel from the boats that bobbed lazily in their slips, their hulls painted in fading shades of blue and red and white. He had made this same walk every morning for nearly forty years, ever since his father had handed him the keys to the family boat and told him, with very few words and a firm nod, that the sea was now his responsibility. Back then, he had been young and eager, his muscles strong and his eyes sharp, capable of reading the water the way some men read books — finding meaning in the subtle shifts of color, the direction of the wind, the way a gull hovered just a little too long over a particular patch of grey-green water. Now his knees ached with the damp cold, his hands were stiff in the mornings, and his eyes, though still keen, were framed by deep lines carved by decades of squinting into the glare of sun on open water. Yet none of that diminished the quiet joy he felt each time he untied the mooring line, felt the gentle push of the current take the bow, and pointed the old vessel toward the open sea, where the world fell away into nothing but sky and water and the occasional cry of a distant bird. There was a kind of freedom out there that he had never been able to find on land, a silence that was not really silence at all but a full and living sound — the slap of waves against the hull, the hum of the engine, the whisper of wind through the rigging — that somehow managed to quiet every worry and every thought that the land seemed so insistent on producing. He had tried to explain this feeling to his children once, and then to his grandchildren, but the words had never come out quite right, and he had seen in their polite, slightly puzzled expressions that they did not understand, and perhaps never would, and he had made his peace with that long ago.
